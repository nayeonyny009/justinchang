Process

CLNx Dashboard Redesign

Improving schedule visibility, navigation clarity, and implementation readiness for a student career dashboard.

Overview

CLNx is a University of Toronto career and co-curricular platform where students book appointments, register for events and workshops, and access career resources. Although the platform offers many useful services, its dashboard made common tasks difficult by mixing personal schedules, public events, repeated navigation, and unrelated content in the same space.

This redesign focused on one core student need: quickly checking upcoming appointments, events, and workshops after logging in.

My Role

UX Designer — User Journey Mapping & Developer Handoff

I led the user journey mapping for the core scheduling tasks and created developer handoff annotations for layout, responsiveness, and semantic HTML. I also participated in the heuristic audit and contributed to selected wireframes, including the My Bookings flow and Resume Critique overlay.

Problem

The dashboard made simple scheduling tasks feel difficult.

Students were not struggling because CLNx lacked useful features. They struggled because the dashboard made those features difficult to recognize and access.

The original layout mixed personal schedules, public events, repeated navigation, and unrelated announcements. As a result, students had to scan, scroll, and guess where to find their own appointments or events.

Research

Heuristic Audit

Through heuristic evaluation, we identified three major usability issues affecting the student scheduling flow.

Cluttered hierarchy

Multiple navigation systems, large visuals, and competing content made it difficult to identify the primary task.

Confusing labels

Labels such as My Schedule, My Calendar, Appointments, Events, and Upcoming Events overlapped in meaning.

Broken content

Some links led to broken or outdated pages, reducing trust and interrupting task completion.

User Journey

Mapping the Sad Path

I mapped two high-frequency student tasks: checking upcoming appointments and trying to change an appointment from in-person to virtual. These journeys helped reveal where students experienced hesitation, confusion, and unmet expectations.

The most important insight was that the schedule was not necessarily missing. It was difficult to discover, difficult to interpret, and separated across confusing sections.

Key Insights

What the Journey Revealed

The schedule existed, but was easy to miss

Users expected one central place for their bookings, but schedule information was split across multiple areas.

Similar labels created hesitation

My Schedule, My Calendar, Appointments, and Events sounded related but behaved differently.

Students expected more control

Users expected to manage appointment details inside CLNx, such as changing attendance format, but the flow did not clearly support this.

Scrolling replaced structure

Important information was pushed below unrelated content, making a simple check feel unnecessarily slow.

Design Direction

Turning Findings into Goals

Based on the audit and journey maps, the redesign focused on making the dashboard more task-oriented while keeping the original platform purpose intact.

Clarify hierarchy

Make the student’s personal schedule visible earlier and easier to scan.

Separate personal and public content

Distinguish booked appointments and events from general opportunities or recommendations.

Reduce navigation ambiguity

Simplify how users move between dashboard content, bookings, and account-related actions.

Wireframes

Rebuilding the Dashboard Flow

The mid-fidelity wireframes reorganized CLNx around the student’s core scheduling needs. Instead of hiding personal bookings below unrelated content, the redesigned dashboard surfaces schedule information, personalized events, and relevant career actions earlier in the experience.

I supported the wireframing process and contributed selected screens, with a focus on My Bookings and appointment-related interaction states.

Interaction Flow

Supporting Bookings and Appointment Actions

Because the journey map showed that students needed more than visibility, we extended the redesign into booking-related states. The My Bookings and Resume Critique overlay screens explored how students could review appointments, understand event details, and act on relevant opportunities.

Developer Handoff

Making the Design Code-Ready

I created developer handoff annotations to explain how the redesigned layout could be implemented using HTML and CSS. These annotations focused on responsive layout behavior, semantic structure, and component-level CSS strategy.

Layout strategy

CSS Grid and Flexbox annotations clarified how dashboard columns, cards, and navigation should be structured.

Responsive behavior

Breakpoint notes explained how multi-column layouts collapse into single-column mobile views.

Semantic HTML

Handoff notes identified where elements should use header, nav, section, article, aside, and figure.

Reflection

This project helped me understand how usability problems often come from structure, wording, and discoverability rather than visual design alone. A page can contain the right features but still fail if users cannot recognize where to go or what each section means.

What I Would Improve Next

If given more time, I would validate the redesigned flow through usability testing with students and compare whether the new dashboard reduces hesitation when finding upcoming bookings. I would also refine the appointment management flow to better support actions students expect, such as changing attendance format.
